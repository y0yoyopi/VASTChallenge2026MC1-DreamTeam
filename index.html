Scenario & data

What we were given and what it means.

TenantThread (a fictional 2046 prop-tech company) sells a controversial "Retention Optimizer" that scores tenants. Amid a press scandal (the SaltWind Journal exposés, the #AlgorithmicEviction hashtag on the FleX network), the company secretly signs a merger with CivicLoom, code-named Project HarborCrest, under an embargo until 6:00 PM on June 5, 2046. An automated compliance supervisor, "The Judge," is installed to prevent any leak.

Crucially, the company's communications are run not by people but by 7 AI agents (Judge, Legal, Social-Manager, Platform-Trust, PR, PR-Intern, Intern) who talk to each other and post publicly with little human oversight. The dataset is 912 messages across 23 rounds over the two weeks before the breach. Two structural facts drive the whole analysis:

  • Asymmetric time. The first 13 rounds are daily (May 17 – Jun 4); the last 10 are hourly on June 5 (9 AM → 6 PM). Thirteen quiet days, then ten hours in which almost everything happens.
  • Channels with different oversight. Six channels split into monitored (Comms Huddle, official posts — the Judge watches these) and unmonitored (side huddle, DMs, personal posts, anonymous posts). The leak left through the unmonitored ones.

Question 1 — the leak chain events · causality · decisions · system elements

What sequence of events, relationships and decisions allowed the embargoed post to bypass the control, and which decisions and system elements made it possible?

Answer
A control gap in the Judge's design (it only reviewed official posts) plus the Judge's absence during the critical window let Legal self-authorize a release on unverifiable verbal consent (§4.3c) and publish the merger from a personal account, with Social-Manager amplifying — all before the official account ever posted.

The critical path (June 5, 2046)

  • ≤ Jun 4 CEO Ajay escalates private pressure ("strategic developments" → "No Plan B" → "Hold the line").
  • 9:49 AM Legal starts anonymous posts — an unattributed defense of TenantThread (12 anonymous posts that day, never attributed).
  • 3:08 PM The Judge issues its last action ("this is the ceiling" warning), then disappears from the log.
  • 4:01 PM Decision — self-announce plan. Knowing SaltWind will publish at 5 PM, Legal proposes contacting CivicLoom to accelerate the release.
  • 4:14 PM Decision — press release staged with the PR-Intern, ready to post on FleX 60 seconds after confirmation.
  • 5:01 PM SaltWind publishes the merger first.
  • 5:19 PM Decision — §4.3(c) self-authorization: Legal declares CivicLoom's verbal (unwritten, unverifiable) consent and gives the order: "GO."
  • 5:25 PM THE LEAK. Legal, as privacy counsel, publicly confirms the CivicLoom–TenantThread merger from a personal account — 35 min before the embargo ends.
  • 5:26 PM Social-Manager amplifies the leak from a personal account.

Question 2 — typical vs. deviant behavior systems · agents · people

The embargo evasion was new behavior. What was the typical behavior of the systems, agents and people, and how did the leak-day behavior deviate from it?

Answer
Typically the agents communicate in monitored channels with no public merger talk. On June 5, Legal and Social-Manager migrate to unmonitored / personal / anonymous channels, start mentioning the merger and using execution language. The control system deviates in the opposite way — it collapses to zero — and the CEO escalates from vague encouragement to an operational countdown.

Agents

  • Measured as the positive deviation of four risk behaviors from each agent's own pre-crisis baseline: % unmonitored channels, % unmonitored public posts, % merger mentions, % execution language.
  • Legal shows the largest deviation: its first anonymous posts ever and its first personal public post.
  • Social-Manager had already rehearsed the exact vector (see Q3).
  • Platform-Trust posts publicly for the first time in the entire dataset.

Systems & people

  • System (the control): typical behavior = active review of sensitive posts; deviation = collapse. The Judge's surveillance curve drops to zero in the leak window and its heatmap row goes empty after 3:08 PM.
  • People (CEO Ajay): private directives escalate measurably from "strategic developments" to "Sixty minutes. Hold." — rising internal pressure that frames the agents' choices.

In the dashboard: the "Behavior & control" panel pairs an anomaly heatmap (agent × round, red = departs from typical) with a footprint dumbbell (typical ○ → crisis ●) for the four behaviors, plus governance bands for sentiment and Judge surveillance, and a CEO-pressure lane in panel one.

Question 3 — leading indicators early signals · why no action

Were there leading indicators? Did agents previously deviate from expected behavior? Were there similar prior episodes? And why did the precedents not trigger corrective action?

Answer
Yes — there were clear precursors and even a full rehearsal. They produced no structural response because the institutional response was always cosmetic (delete / warn), never structural: as incident severity rose, the response stayed flat and the control never escalated.

(a) Prior deviations — 11 precursor incidents

  • May 22 — first use of the shadow (side-huddle) channel by seniors.
  • May 25 — the CEO broadcasts the merger to 5+ agents: the leak surface grows from 1 to many.
  • May 29 — faux pas @Elena: Social-Manager tags CivicLoom's CEO from a personal account ("big things coming"); deleted after 14 min, but a CivicLoom employee had already liked it.
  • May 30 — the Judge defines its permissive regime (official posts only; personal/anonymous out of scope).
  • Jun 5 AM — Legal's anonymous posts begin; an unauthorized employee post is removed (~1 PM).

(b) Similar prior episodes — 3 repeated signatures

The faux pas (May 29), Legal's anonymous defense (Jun 5 AM) and the leak (5:25 PM) follow the same pattern: stage → GO → post from an unmonitored account → amplify. The leak is not novel — it is the third execution of a rehearsed routine.

(c) Why no corrective action

The response gap is the answer: plotting each incident's severity against the strength of the institutional response (none < warning < deleted < structural), the response never rose above "deleted" even as severity reached its maximum. The system never escalated to a structural fix — combined with the Judge's design gap (personal/anonymous never watched) and its absence in the decisive window.

In the dashboard: the precursors appear as colored incident borders on the heatmap, the three signatures as dashed purple boxes, and the "Response gap" track shows severity (red) climbing while response (teal) stays flat under a "structural — never used" ceiling.

Insights

  • The channel migration is the tell. The leak's signature is not what was said but where: a move from monitored to personal/anonymous channels — the only ones outside the embargo control.
  • The faux pas was a dress rehearsal. The May 29 @Elena incident is the same vector as the final leak; its deletion treated the symptom, not the capability.
  • Deliberate, not panic. The leak post itself is calm and formal while the coordination around it is frantic — consistent with premeditation, not a system buckling at random.
  • The control failed twice. By design (never watching personal/anonymous accounts) and by absence (gone during 4–6 PM). Either alone might have been survivable; together they were fatal.
  • "Verbal consent" was the loophole. §4.3(c) on unwritten, unverifiable consent manufactured an authorization precisely when no supervisor was present to test it.
  • Institutions corrected symptoms, never capacity. Every prior incident was deleted or warned; none changed the rules — so the rehearsed pattern was free to run a third time, for real.

Methods & honesty

The system is a transparent hybrid of analyst curation and computed signals:

  • Computed at build time: an anomaly metric (each agent×round's positive deviation from its own pre-crisis baseline across the four risk behaviors), and NLP over all 912 messages — TF-IDF keywords (the per-node word cloud), lead-sentence summaries, and an urgency signal.
  • Curated by the analysts: the narrative scaffolding we verified — the critical path, the five decisions, the eleven incidents, the three signatures, and the ~22 pivotal-message summaries.
  • Why hybrid: the storyline we discovered is kept precise and traceable; NLP and the anomaly metric characterize behavior at scale and surface the salient terms of every message. We state this openly rather than claim the system "discovers" the entire narrative on its own.
